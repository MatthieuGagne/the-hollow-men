# The Bureau

Inspired by the Babylon 5 Psi-Corps, filtered through the world's noir/cyberpunk/Lovecraftian DNA. The Bureau doesn't feel like the Psi-Corps — it feels like what would grow in *this* soil. Corporate-regulatory, not military. The indoctrination is professional formation, not chanting. The mechanisms route through sigils and ley architecture. The ideology is embedded in career structure, not cult.

---

## Section 1: The Bureau as Total Institution

### Detection

The ley terminals throughout NOX are passive scanners. That's their secondary function — the primary function is harvest, but harvest infrastructure reads output signatures as a side effect. Any practitioner spending significant time near a terminal generates a detectable pattern. Children are near terminals constantly — schools, transit hubs, the Promenade. The Bureau gets a ping when the signature crosses a threshold.

This is not a secret. It's in the founding charter, same paragraph as the name. *The Bureau maintains a registry of all persons exhibiting licensed ability for purposes of public safety and orderly administration.* Nobody reads the founding charter.

### The Academies

Bureau Academies are good schools. This is important. Not punitive, not Dickensian — *excellent*. Better facilities than anything in the Sprawl. The Bureau funds them because the Bureau has resources. Parents in the Heights see it as an honor. Parents in the Sprawl see it as a lifeline.

The curriculum embeds the ideology through professional formation, not indoctrination. The framing: ability is a gift, gifts carry obligation, the Bureau is the steward of that obligation. Magic is presented as inherently civic — something that exists in relationship to the world around you, something that *needs* the framework to be used responsibly. The ley architecture is infrastructure. Sigils are tools. The License is what separates a practitioner from a liability.

Nobody in the Academies is teaching a lie, exactly. They're teaching an interpretation. The interpretation happens to serve the harvest. The teachers mostly believe it.

### What It Produces

A licensed practitioner who came up through the Academy doesn't experience the Bureau as external constraint. It's identity. Their cohort is their cohort. Their Grade is their professional self-image. The institutions that shaped them were good to them, and the ideology they carry isn't brainwashing — it's just what they were taught, by people they trusted, who were also just taught.

This is what makes the Paladin's arc heartbreaking. They're not wrong to believe in justice. They believe in the only institution that ever told them they mattered.

### Difference from Psi-Corps

Psi-Corps makes telepathy feel like a mutation that society fears and the Corps manages. The Bureau makes magic feel like a *profession* that society values and the Bureau licenses. The horror is different: not persecution dressed as protection, but genuine institutional care optimized for harvest. The teachers believed it. The administrators believe it. Most of the enforcers believe it. The evil is structural, and the structure is genuinely good at some things it claims to be good at — which is worse.

## Section 2: The Grade System

### What It Measures

Formal assessment happens at the Academy, and again at licensing. The Bureau measures **Resonance Index** — technically, how efficiently a practitioner's output interfaces with the ley architecture. The Bureau presents this as a measure of ability strength and precision. Both descriptions are accurate. Neither is the whole truth. High Resonance Index means more powerful practitioner. It also means more harvestable output. The Bureau conflates these deliberately, and most practitioners never separate them.

Resonance Index is expressed as a **Grade**, I through V. The designation is public — on the license, in employment contracts, in the fine print of every sigil sale. Some practitioners put it in their professional signatures. It is experienced as a mark of distinction. It functions as a meter reading.

### The Grades

| Grade | Who | Bureau Relationship |
|-------|-----|---------------------|
| **I** | Detectable output, below threshold for active practice. Most people with any measurable ability. | Register and report changes. No active obligations. The Bureau ignores you unless you draw attention. |
| **II** | Standard practitioners. The bulk of the licensed working tier — Promenade shops, corporate mages, street-level professionals. | Annual assessment. Standard sigil assignment. Bureau employment available but not expected. This is the normal life. |
| **III** | Elevated output. Likely Academy-trained. Access to more powerful sigils, more complex techniques. | Quarterly assessment. Career guidance toward Bureau-adjacent roles. The Bureau starts having opinions about your employer. |
| **IV** | High-output. Rare. The Bureau takes a personal interest early — Grade IV children get the best Academy placement, the most attentive instructors, the clearest path to Bureau careers. | Mandatory Bureau reporting structure. Non-Bureau employment requires approval. The framing is prestige. The function is control. |
| **V** | Exceptional. The Bureau doesn't publish how many exist. Summoners sometimes test at or near Grade V — or off the scale in ways the standard assessment doesn't know how to categorize. | Classified. The public record stops here. What the Bureau does with Grade V practitioners is not documented in anything Reid has ever had access to. |

### Social Texture

Higher Grade is genuinely prestigious — the Bureau made sure of that. Grade IV practitioners are respected, earn more, move through the Heights with a different ease. The ideology is load-bearing: greater output means greater civic responsibility, and the Bureau is how that responsibility gets channeled into something useful. This is not experienced as exploitation. It's experienced as vocation.

The trap is elegant: the practitioners the Bureau harvests most efficiently are also the ones who identify most deeply with the Bureau. Grade IVs don't resent the system. They *are* the system.

### Exceptions — What the System Can't Read

**Mara** has no Grade. No assessment, no file, no rating. The ley terminals never got a clean read — she was never near one long enough, and nobody sent the paperwork. Her output isn't lower than a Grade IV. It's *unmetered*, which is different. The system has no category for that. When a Bureau assessor eventually encounters her, the instrument returns an error before a number.

**The Martial Artist** has no Grade for the opposite reason. Their output loops inward — it never reaches the ley architecture. The Bureau's assessment protocol requires outward resonance to function. They get a reading of zero, filed as "non-practitioner," forgotten. The Martial Artist knows this. They counted on it.

**Summoners** are the anomaly the system was designed to handle carefully. Their output signature doesn't read cleanly as a single Index — there's a doubling, a resonance that doesn't come from one source. The Bureau's official position is that Summoner output is "amplified through the bonded being," fitting their mythology about externally-channeled magic. The unofficial position, held by anyone who's done the math, is that the numbers don't add up.

### Character Implications

- **The Black Mage** — almost certainly Grade III or IV. Academy-trained, career structured around their rating. Their arc isn't just defecting from the system. It's defecting from the thing that told them who they were.
- **The Paladin** — Grade II or solid III. Capable enough for enforcement, not so exceptional the Bureau directed them toward research. The institution worked for them at the level they were given access to.
- **Vesper** — rated high enough that the Bureau monitored her closely. When she started following the wrong thread, they noticed quickly. The order to disappear her came from someone who understood what a loose Grade IV or V Summoner represented.
- **Reid** — no Grade. Fighter, not a practitioner. Twenty years working beside Grade II and III enforcers. Knows exactly what the system looks like from outside it.

## Section 3: The Assessment Division

### What They're Called

Official designation: **Bureau Assessment Division**. Street name: **the Grays**. They wear gray instead of Bureau blue — officially, to mark their separate warrant authority. In practice: when you see gray in a doorway, you don't wait to find out which kind of assessment this is.

### What They Do

- Hunt unlicensed practitioners — anyone operating without a Grade on file
- Investigate Grade fraud — practitioners suspected of operating above their registered level
- Handle "output irregularities" — the Bureau's language for a signature that suggests jailbroken sigils, unauthorized Meridian contact, or Ruins exposure
- Manage Grade V situations — the file on what this means is not public

The Grays can enter without standard law enforcement warrants if they can document output irregularity. The legal threshold for that documentation is low. Regular Bureau enforcers need warrants. The Grays need a reading.

### Who They Are

The Assessment Division recruits from Academy graduates who tested Grade III or above. Their internal culture treats this as self-evident: you need high output to detect and evaluate high output. An Assessor can read a practitioner's signature at close range the way a trained ear identifies an instrument in a chord. They know what a Bureau sigil feels like. They know what a jailbroken one feels like. They know what it feels like when someone's been somewhere they shouldn't.

They are the Bureau's premium product — the best-formed, most thoroughly shaped graduates of the Academy pipeline. They believe in the Grade system the way people believe in things they've organized their entire identity around. Not dogmatically. Structurally. Remove the Grade system and you remove the framework that makes them exceptional.

They're not cartoonishly evil. The Grays are genuinely skilled, genuinely believe in what they're doing, and are genuinely right that unregistered high-output practitioners create real instability. The Bureau built real dangers into the architecture to justify real enforcement. The Grays respond to those dangers effectively. The horror: they're good at a job that serves something they don't understand.

### Relationship to Regular Bureau Enforcement

Reid was regular Bureau — general law in the Heights. He worked *beside* the Grays occasionally, different chain of command, different warrant authority, different culture. Regular enforcers refer cases to the Grays when output irregularity is involved. The Grays sometimes request muscle from regular enforcement when they need numbers.

That's what happened the night of Vesper. The operation came through Assessment Division channels. Reid's team was seconded as support — muscle, not authority. He was there because the Grays needed bodies, not because he was one of them.

This distinction matters: when Reid refused the order and let Vesper walk, he was regular enforcement committing insubordination. If an Assessor had refused, the response would have been different and faster. The Bureau stripped Reid and put him under watch. An Assessor who did what Reid did would not be put under watch. There would be a file update and a notification to next of kin.

### What Reid Carries

He's seen how the Grays operate from the inside of one operation. He knows their protocols — how they contain a location, how they communicate internally, what they do when a target runs. He also knows something most people don't: the Grays aren't given full operational context. The team that came for Vesper didn't know why. They had a designation — *output irregularity, potential Grade escalation, Bureau security concern* — and orders. The Assessor leading the team had a warrant and a reading and a job to do.

The order came from above the team. From someone who knew what Vesper had found. That someone is not a Gray. The Grays are instruments. Reid has spent years trying to figure out whose hand holds them.

## Section 4: Calibration

### What the Bureau Calls It

**Output Calibration Procedure.** Shortened to *calibration* in official use. The Bureau has a diagnostic code for it. There's a clinical pathway, a consent form, a follow-up schedule. It is presented as a medical procedure.

Street term: *calibrated*. Used as a past tense that nobody wants applied to them. "They calibrated her last spring." The flatness of it is the horror.

### What It Physically Is

Standard sigils slot in and out — flesh or hardware, interchangeable by design. A calibration sigil is different. It's **deep-bonded** — anchored below the interface layer the practitioner can reach, integrated directly into their ley architecture connection at a point that can't be accessed from outside a Bureau facility. It doesn't skim the tithe the way a licensed sigil does. It routes substantially all output through the ley architecture, leaving only enough residual for the practitioner to function as normal. Normal meaning: no abilities. No output the Bureau hasn't already taken. Enough left to pass as a person.

The procedure requires a Bureau-certified augmentation specialist and a Grade III or above Assessor to supervise the bonding. It takes about forty minutes. Patients are awake for it.

### The Diagnostic Framing

*Output Calibration is the recommended clinical intervention for practitioners exhibiting documented instability, irregular resonance patterns, or output events posing risk to ley architecture integrity.*

The Bureau employs practitioners who administer this procedure and believe they are helping. The diagnostic category is real — there are cases where uncontrolled high-output produces genuine instability, damages ley terminals, puts other practitioners at risk. The Bureau built these cases into the architecture. The danger is real enough that the intervention feels justified.

The lie embedded in the framing: magic is presented as external, borrowed, conditionally held. If your power flows from the ley architecture in the first place, routing it back there is just restoring balance. The Bureau's foundational myth about the origin of power is what makes calibration feel defensible. Without that lie, calibration is reaching into someone and taking what was always theirs.

### What It Feels Like

Practitioners who've been calibrated describe it the same way, consistently enough that the account has traveled through the underground as a warning:

*Like a quiet. Not pain. Not absence. More like something that was always moving — you never noticed it until it stopped.*

The output doesn't disappear. That's the part nobody tells you. It's still there, still flowing — you can feel it. You just can't reach it anymore. It moves through you and away from you. You can feel the direction. After a while most people stop feeling it. The ones who don't stop are the ones who end up in the Meridian looking for something the black market may not actually have.

### Who Gets Calibrated

- Practitioners who refused compliance and were caught
- Grade V assessments where the Bureau determined employment was inadvisable
- Practitioners whose output signature changed in ways suggesting jailbreaking, unauthorized Meridian contact, or Ruins exposure
- Practitioners who asked the wrong questions with enough persistence to generate an Assessment Division file

The Bureau does not advertise the criteria. The criteria are whatever the Bureau decides they are. The consent form exists. Consent is not always what the form implies.

### Can It Be Reversed

Officially: no. The Bureau's position is that deep-bonded calibration is permanent and that reversal attempts carry severe risk of ley architecture damage and practitioner harm.

Unofficially: the jailbreaking community has theories. Mara has heard things — a name, a district, an incomplete account of something that happened in the Warrens that may or may not have worked. Nobody has done it cleanly. The difference between *nobody has done it cleanly* and *it cannot be done* is the difference between a closed door and a locked one.

### Character Implications

- **The Occultist** — their classified Bureau file. An attempted calibration that didn't take? A procedure that produced results the Bureau's documentation has no category for? Their entity-touched abilities suggest exposure to something the Bureau tried to close off and couldn't.
- **The Black Mage** — swapping to a jailbroken sigil isn't just defection. It's running before the other option becomes available. They know what happens to Grade III and IV practitioners who step out of line. They've known someone who got calibrated. The swap is an act of preemptive self-preservation that becomes ideology in the doing.
- **Reid** — twenty years as Bureau enforcement. He's seen calibrated practitioners. People the Grays processed before he understood what the procedure was. He filed the paperwork. He didn't ask questions. That's in him somewhere.
